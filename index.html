Chapter 01

Introduction

Completed
1

Background

Overview of sign language as a communication method and why the Deaf/hard-of-hearing (HoH) community in Palestine needs a dedicated digital learning tool.

2

Problem Statement

No app currently teaches Palestinian Sign Language (PSL) specifically. Existing regional apps (Mushir, Esharti) cover general Arabic Sign Language only, and none offer camera-verified production practice.

3

Motivation

Scale of the need: a significant Deaf/HoH population in Palestine, worsened post-conflict disability rates in Gaza, and a complete absence of PSL vocabulary datasets or scalable tools.

4

Objectives

What the project sets out to build: an interactive learning platform, an original PSL word dataset, and a working sign-recognition pipeline.

5

Scope & Limitations

Defines project boundaries: Alphabet + Numbers + 30–40 original words only. A wider vocabulary is explicitly future work, not a deliverable of this project.

6

Expected Contributions & Benefits

The first PSL-specific dataset, the first camera-verified PSL learning tool, and an accessible, scalable web-based format.

Chapter 04 · Dataset Track

Dataset Design & Construction

Upcoming
This chapter gives your original PSL dataset a dedicated home — treated as a research contribution in its own right, not a side note.
1

Dataset Strategy

Justifies the two-track approach: an existing public dataset for Alphabet/Numbers (fast MVP) vs. an original filmed dataset for 30–40 words, since no PSL data exists for these.

2

Signer Recruitment

How signers were sourced, selection criteria (fluency, diversity), and why signer variety matters for model generalization.

3

Consent & Ethics

Explicit likeness-use agreements signed by signers; extra caution justified by likely use by minors; alignment with the platform's privacy-first design.

4

Data Collection Protocol

Filming setup: multiple angles, slow-motion capture, consistent lighting/background, and number of repetitions per sign — explaining why this improves classifier robustness.

5

Labeling & Annotation

Labeling scheme (sign name, category, signer ID, angle), tools used, and quality-control steps such as cross-checking labels.

6

Keypoint Extraction Pipeline

Using MediaPipe Hands to convert raw video into keypoint coordinates; explains why keypoints — not pixels — are used: smaller data, privacy-preserving, faster inference.

7

Dataset Structure & Storage

Final schema (per-sign keypoint sequences + labels), train/validation/test split ratios, dataset size/statistics, and how raw video is discarded or securely isolated after keypoint extraction.

Chapter 06 · Model Track

Model Building & Training

Upcoming
The machine-learning counterpart to Chapter 4 — this is where the model-building work belongs, using the dataset produced there.
1

Model Selection Rationale

Why a lightweight classifier on keypoints was chosen over heavy video-based CNNs — driven by the no-GPU constraint and the need for browser-side inference.

2

Feature Engineering

Normalizing keypoint coordinates for scale/position invariance, and possibly encoding motion/sequence for dynamic signs vs. static ones (alphabet vs. words).

3

Training Setup

Google Colab environment, chosen framework (e.g. TensorFlow/PyTorch), the train/validation/test split from Chapter 4's dataset, and hyperparameter choices.

4

Training Process

Separate training runs for (a) the public Alphabet/Numbers dataset and (b) the original 30–40 word dataset, explaining why they may need different handling — static pose vs. word-level gesture sequences.

5

Evaluation Metrics

Accuracy, confusion matrix, per-sign performance breakdown — with emphasis on why false-pass and false-fail rates matter specifically for a pass/fail learning gate.

6

Model Optimization

Any tuning done to reduce model size/latency for in-browser deployment, such as quantization, pruning, or architecture simplification.

7

Deployment / Inference

Exporting the trained model to a browser-compatible format and integrating it into the recognition pipeline described in Chapter 5.
